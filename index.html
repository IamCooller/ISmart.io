    About the book

    This biography is based on conversations with Steve Jobs himself, as well as with his relatives, friends, enemies, rivals and colleagues. Jobs had no control over the author. He openly answered all questions and expected the same honesty from the rest. This is a story about a life full of ups and downs, about a strong man and a talented businessman who was one of the first to understand: to succeed in the 21st century, you need to combine creativity and technology.

    Authors

    Walter Isaacson - American journalist, writer and biographer; author of popular biographies of Steve Jobs, Henry Kissinger, Benjamin Franklin, Albert Einstein and Leonardo da Vinci. President of the Aspen Institute. Former director of CNN Broadcasting Company and chief editor of Time magazine.

    Why you need to read this book

    Praise to the madmen. The rebels. The troublemakers. To the losers. Those who are always out of place and out of place. Those who see the world differently. They do not follow the rules. They laugh at the foundations. They can be quoted, argued with, praised or cursed. But just ignoring them is impossible. After all, they bring change. They push humanity forward. And let someone say: crazy, we say: geniuses. After all, only a madman believes that he is able to change the world, and therefore changes it.

    Foreword: How did this book come about?

    In early summer 2004, Steve Jobs called me. Friendliness periodically happened over the years of our acquaintance with him, especially when he planned to release a new product and wanted me to put a photo on the cover of Time magazine or talk about the CNN novelty, where I worked then. But since I no longer worked there or there, Steve showed up infrequently. We chatted about the Aspen Institute, where I recently moved to work, and I invited him to give a speech at our summer camp in Colorado. Steve replied that he would come with joy, but would not speak. Instead, he suggested walking and talking.

    And then I decided to write his biography. Jobs promised that he would not control me, would not even ask me to show the manuscript, which surprised me a lot. “This is your book,” he said. “I won’t even read it.” But in the fall, he seems to have abandoned the idea of ​​a book. I did not know then that he had an exacerbation of the disease. Steve stopped answering calls, and I postponed the project for a while.

    Jobs unexpectedly called me on New Year's Eve, 2009. He was at home in Palo Alto with his sister, writer Mona Simpson. My wife and children went skiing, and Steve could not accompany them for health reasons. Steve was in a philosophical mood; we talked for more than an hour. He recalled how at the age of twelve he decided to build a digital frequency counter, found the phone number of Bill Hewlett, the founder of HP in the directory, called him and asked him to send the necessary details. He said that the past twelve years, since returning to Apple, have been the most effective in his life: many new products have been created. But much more important, Steve admitted, for him to repeat what Hewlett and his friend David Packard once managed: they founded a company that turned out to be so innovative and creative that it outlived its creators.

    “As a kid, I considered myself a humanist, but I always liked electronics,” Steve recalled. - Once I read what one of my idols, Edwin Land of Polaroid, writes about how important people are who solve problems at the intersection of the humanities and the exact sciences. And I decided that this is exactly what I want to do. ” Steve seemed to throw me ideas for a book (by the way, in this case, the tip turned out to be useful). When I worked on the biographies of Franklin and Einstein, I was most interested in creativity in people in which the love of the humanities is combined with the ability to study exact sciences. Moreover, I am sure that this is the key to creating an innovative economy of the 21st century.
